What is GRC, in plain English

This whole project is an example of GRC, which stands for Governance, Risk, and Compliance. Those are three connected jobs that keep an organization safe and trustworthy.

  • Governance is making the rules and deciding who is in charge. Think of the rulebook and the referees for a sports team. Without it, everyone plays by their own rules and no one knows who is responsible when something breaks.
  • Risk is figuring out what could go wrong, how likely it is, and how bad it would be. Think of a weather forecast: the chance of a storm times how strong it might be. A risk score is the chance of a bad event multiplied by the damage it would cause.
  • Compliance is proving you actually follow the rules that apply to you. Think of a restaurant health inspection. Saying the kitchen is clean is not enough; you show the logs and the temperatures. Compliance is the evidence behind your promises.

A few short terms you will meet, defined simply. NIST is the National Institute of Standards and Technology, a United States government body that publishes trusted security guides. CSF is its Cybersecurity Framework. MFA is Multi-Factor Authentication: logging in with more than a password, such as a password plus a phone code. ePHI is electronic Protected Health Information, meaning digital medical data. POA&M is a Plan of Action and Milestones, a to-do list of fixes with owners and dates. SOC 2, ISO 27001, and HIPAA are three security or privacy rulebooks. TPRM is Third-Party Risk Management: checking the outside vendors you rely on. Each term is defined again the first time it appears in a section, so you never need to memorize this list.

Tab-by-tab tour

Each part below matches one section of the page, in order. For each: what you see, what to do, the one fact worth remembering, a plain comparison, and why it matters to you as someone evaluating the candidate.

Posture (the maturity radar)

What you see: a six-sided radar chart and six scores. These are the Functions of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover, each scored 1 to 4.

What to do: read the shape. The further a point reaches out, the stronger that area.

The one fact: the overall score is 1.57 out of 4.0, which is Tier 2 (Risk Informed). Protect is strongest at 2.00. Detect and Respond tie for weakest at 1.33 each.

Plain comparison: a report card for the whole security program. It shows this company protects itself fairly well but is poor at noticing and reacting to attacks.

Why this matters: starting with an honest overall grade, bad parts included, shows judgment. The weakest areas are named first and fixed first.

Risk (the 5 by 5 matrix)

What you see: a grid five squares wide and five tall, with numbered dots. Side to side is likelihood; up and down is impact. A side card shows the selected risk's details.

What to do: hover, tap, or use the keyboard to select a dot. The card updates.

The one fact: there are ten risks. The biggest is R-001, a shared cloud administrator account with no Multi-Factor Authentication (password-only login). Likelihood 4 of 5 times impact 5 of 5 equals a score of 20, the highest, rated Critical. The owner is the CTO; it maps to control PR.AA-01.

Plain comparison: a weather map for danger. The top-right corner is the hurricane zone, where storms are both likely and severe. R-001 sits there.

Why this matters: scoring risk as likelihood times impact is the standard method, and putting an owner on each risk shows accountability.

Controls (design versus operating)

What you see: cards, each with a control and two test results, design and operating.

What to do: read both results. Design asks if the control is correct on paper. Operating asks if it actually works in real life.

The one fact: control TEST-04 is the incident-response plan. It passes design because the written plan exists. It fails operating because the plan was never practiced.

Plain comparison: owning a fire extinguisher versus knowing how to use it. The one on the wall passes design. If no one has ever sprayed it, it fails operating, and in an emergency only operating matters.

Why this matters: testing both shows the candidate knows a rule on paper is worthless if it does not work when it counts.

POA&M (the repair plan)

What you see: a timeline where each bar is one scheduled fix. POA&M means Plan of Action and Milestones, a to-do list of fixes with owners and dates.

What to do: read top to bottom. The most urgent items come first.

The one fact: ten tracked repair items, each with an owner and a due date. Critical access and detection gaps close first; lower-priority polish lands last.

Plain comparison: a home-renovation schedule. Fix the leaking roof before repainting the guest room. Order matters, and every job gets a name and a deadline.

Why this matters: finding problems is easy. Sequencing fixes by severity, assigning owners, and setting dates is the part employers pay for.

Crosswalk (one control, four rulebooks)

What you see: a table with tabs. Each row is a control, and the columns show how that one control maps to four security frameworks at once.

What to do: click the tabs to filter, and read across one row.

The one fact: one Multi-Factor Authentication control maps to all four frameworks at the same time: CSF PR.AA-01, NIST 800-53 IA-2, ISO 27001 A.5.17, SOC 2 CC6.1, and HIPAA 164.312(a)(2)(i).

Plain comparison: learning that one good habit, brushing your teeth, satisfies your dentist, your doctor, and your own goal of fresh breath. You do the work once and check several boxes.

Why this matters: this is the money-saving argument of a unified compliance program, avoiding duplicate work across frameworks.

Third-Party Risk (checking vendors)

What you see: a four-level vendor ranking, a review process, questionnaire topics, and one worked example. This is TPRM, Third-Party Risk Management: judging the safety of outside companies you depend on.

What to do: read the four tiers, then the worked example.

The one fact: the example vendor, MedStream Analytics, would handle electronic Protected Health Information (digital medical data) with production-like access, making it Tier 1, the highest risk. Before any data flows, the company must sign a Business Associate Agreement (the required healthcare-data contract) and review the vendor's SOC 2 report.

Plain comparison: a background check before handing over your house keys. The more sensitive the access, the deeper the check.

Why this matters: most modern breaches come through a trusted vendor, not the front door. Screening by potential damage shows where real risk lives.

Running the live demo yourself

No technical skill is needed. Follow these steps in the Demo section.

  1. Click API health check. This asks the server if it is awake. You should see a healthy response and a green status indicator, like pinging a friend to see if they are online.
  2. Click Recalculate maturity to 1.57. The server adds up all the scores and computes the overall grade again from scratch, using the same data. Watch it return 1.57, proving the headline number is calculated, not hand-typed.
  3. In the vendor form, leave the default name MedStream Analytics and submit. The server returns the risk tier, which should be Tier 1, Critical, with a Business Associate Agreement required.

A live backend that answers in real time shows the work is reproducible: anyone can re-run the math and get the same answer, which a static PDF cannot do. If the server sleeps, the page still works from bundled data and tells you it is in fallback mode.
